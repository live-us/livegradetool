LSAC GPA Grading Scale 2026: Why Your 4.0 Might Not Be a 4.0

If you’re heading toward law school, don't get too attached to the GPA on your college transcript. It’s actually not the number law schools care about most.

Instead, they look at your LSAC GPA. Think of this as a "universal grade" created by the Law School Admission Council. Since some colleges are harder than others or use different grading systems, the LSAC recalculates everyone’s grades using the exact same formula. This keeps things fair for everyone, no matter where you went to school.

I’ll show you exactly how they flip your grades around so you can figure out your real score before you hit "apply."

What Is the LSAC GPA Grading Scale?

Basically, LSAC GPA is a way to put every law school applicant on the same playing field. Even though your college has its own specific rules for grades, LSAC ignores them and recalculates your GPA using their own universal system.

This new score goes into your "CAS report"—think of it as your official application file—and that’s the number every law school will look at when they decide whether to let you in.

Why the LSAC GPA Is Different from Your University GPA

Different universities use different grading systems, which creates a bit of a mess for law schools trying to compare students. For example:

  • The "A+" Problem: Some schools give you a 4.33 for an A+, while others cap everything at a 4.0.
  • Retakes: Some colleges let you replace a bad grade if you retake the class, while others keep both grades on your record.
  • Global Differences: Schools use all sorts of systems, from percentage-based grades to international standards.

Without a universal system, it would be impossible to compare a student from one school to a student from another fairly. That is why LSAC steps in to even the playing field:

  • They use one scale: Everyone is measured against the same 4.33-point system.
  • They count everything: They include every single undergraduate course you've ever taken.
  • They use consistent math: They re-weight your credits so that everyone's GPA is calculated using the exact same logic.

By doing this, law schools can look at your application and know exactly how you stack up against every other applicant, regardless of where you went to school.
