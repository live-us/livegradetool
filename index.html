Why Law Schools Use the LSAC CAS GPA

The LSAC GPA (also known as the Credential Assembly Service or CAS GPA) is the standard metric used by the American Bar Association (ABA) to rank law school applicants. Since every university has a different grading system, the Law School Admission Council standardizes all undergraduate transcripts to ensure a level playing field for all candidates.

Pro Tip: Your undergraduate transcript GPA and your official LSAC GPA will likely differ due to how the council treats retakes and non-punitive withdrawals.

The LSAC GPA Calculation Formula

Our calculator uses the official mathematical methodology provided by LSAC. The formula is:

$$LSAC\ CAS\ GPA = \frac{\sum (LSAC\ Grade\ Value \times Credit\ Hours)}{Total\ Attempted\ Credits}$$

This means that an A+ is weighted at 4.33, providing a significant boost compared to the standard 4.0 scale used by most U.S. colleges and universities.

What Is LSAC GPA (CAS GPA)?

The LSAC GPA, also called the Credential Assembly Service (CAS) GPA, is a standardized GPA calculated from all undergraduate coursework completed before your first bachelor's degree.

Key Definition:

LSAC GPA = A unified GPA based on all graded undergraduate credits across all institutions.

Why It Matters:

  • Law schools do not use your transcript GPA.
  • Your CAS GPA is the official number used in admissions.
  • It is reported to the ABA (American Bar Association) for law school rankings

Why Your LSAC GPA Is Different from Your Transcript GPA

Most applicants notice a difference between their transcript GPA and LSAC GPA. This happens due to strict standardization rules.

Retaken Courses (No Grade Replacement)

  • Universities may replace old grades
  • LSAC includes both attempts

Even if you improve your grade, the original grade still impacts your GPA.

Transfer & Community College Credits

  • All institutions are included
  • Community college grades count

Even grades that never affected your university GPA are included.

A+ Grades (4.33 Scale Advantage)

  • LSAC assigns 4.33 to A+
  • Many universities cap at 4.0

Your LSAC GPA can exceed 4.0.

Pass/Fail & Withdrawals

  • Passing grades (P, CR) excluded
  • Failing grades counted as 0.00
  • Punitive withdrawals (WF, WU, WNP) counted as failing

Academic Forgiveness Policies Ignored

  • Some universities remove low grades
  • LSAC still counts them if visible on transcript

This is a major reason GPAs drop.

LSAC GPA Formula (How It’s Calculated)

Your CAS GPA is calculated using a weighted average based on all applicable undergraduate coursework.

Explanation

  • Convert each grade into LSAC grade points
  • Multiply each grade point by its credit hours
  • Add all grade points together
  • Divide by total graded credits

The challenge is not the mathematical formula itself, it is understanding which credits and grades are included in the calculation.

Formula


    CAS GPA = Σ (Grade Points × Credit Hours)
                ------------------------------
                    Total Graded Credits

Simplified Explanation

  • Grade Points ⊸ Credits = Total Points
  • Total Points ÷ Total Credits = GPA

The complexity is not in the math, it lies in determining which courses are counted under LSAC rules.

What Counts in Your LSAC GPA

LSAC includes a broad range of undergraduate academic records when calculating your CAS GPA. The goal is full standardization across all institutions.

Included in LSAC GPA

  • All undergraduate coursework completed before earning your first bachelor’s degree
  • Courses from every institution attended (all colleges and universities)
  • Repeated courses (both original and repeated attempts are counted)
  • Transfer credits from other institutions
  • Community college coursework
  • Study abroad courses (if recorded on your official transcript)
  • AP / CLEP credits (only if they include grades and credit hours)
  • Credit-bearing practical or physical courses (e.g., PE, ROTC)
  • Punitive withdrawals such as WF, WU, and WNP (treated as failing grades)

What Does NOT Count in LSAC GPA

Certain types of coursework are excluded from LSAC’s CAS GPA calculation. These are removed to maintain standardization across different grading systems.

Excluded from LSAC GPA

  • Courses completed after the first bachelor’s degree is awarded
  • Pass/Fail grades (P, CR, S)
  • Non-credit coursework
  • Remedial or developmental courses (if marked as non-standard)
  • Non-punitive withdrawals (W, WP)
  • Incomplete grades (if later marked non-punitive or not converted to a grade)
  • International coursework (unless officially transferred into a U.S./Canadian transcript)
  • Courses without standardized grading conversion rules in LSAC methodology

Real Scenarios: Why Your LSAC GPA Changes

Understanding real-world cases helps explain why your LSAC CAS GPA often differs from your university transcript GPA.

The difference is not random — it comes from LSAC’s standardized rule-based evaluation system.

Scenario 1: Retake Impact

You fail a course (F) and later retake it and earn an A.

  • Transcript GPA: Only the A is counted (depending on institutional policy)
  • LSAC GPA: Both F (4 credits) and A (4 credits) are included

Result: GPA decreases because the failed attempt is still included in LSAC calculation.

Scenario 2: A+ Boost

Your institution treats A+ the same as A (4.00 GPA).

  • Transcript GPA: A+ = 4.00
  • LSAC GPA: A+ = 4.33 (on 4.33 scale system)

Result: LSAC GPA can exceed 4.0, providing a measurable GPA increase.

Scenario 3: Transfer Credit Impact

You completed coursework at a community college before transferring to a university.

  • Transcript GPA: Transfer grades may be excluded or hidden
  • LSAC GPA: All transferable credits (e.g., 60 credits) are included

Result: GPA often decreases if earlier academic performance was lower.

Scenario 4: Academic Forgiveness Trap

Your university replaces or removes a low grade through an academic forgiveness policy.

  • Transcript GPA: Old grade is removed or excluded
  • LSAC GPA: Old grade is still counted if it appears on the transcript record

Result: Hidden GPA reduction that often surprises applicants during CAS evaluation.

CAS GPA vs Transcript GPA

Your transcript GPA and LSAC CAS GPA are calculated using fundamentally different rules. These differences explain most GPA discrepancies applicants experience.

Factor Transcript GPA LSAC GPA (CAS)
Retakes Replaced or excluded (institution-dependent) Both attempts are counted
Transfer Credits Sometimes excluded Always included if credit-bearing
A+ Scale Often capped at 4.0 Up to 4.33 on LSAC scale
Academic Forgiveness Applied by university policies Ignored if grade exists on transcript

These structural differences explain the majority of unexpected changes between institutional GPA and LSAC CAS GPA.

Minimum Credit Rule (Important)

LSAC applies a minimum credit threshold to ensure sufficient academic data for a valid CAS GPA calculation.

60-Credit Requirement

  • LSAC requires a minimum of 60 graded semester credits
  • If total graded credits are below this threshold:
    • No numeric CAS GPA is calculated
    • Only a qualitative academic summary is provided

Quarter Credits Conversion

Some institutions use a quarter system instead of semester hours. LSAC standardizes these credits before calculating CAS GPA.

Conversion Rule


Quarter Credits ⊸ 0.67 = Semester Credits (LSAC Equivalent)

Explanation

  • Quarter credits are multiplied by 0.67
  • This converts them into LSAC-equivalent semester credits
  • Converted credits are then included in CAS GPA calculation

Example

  • 4 quarter credits → 2.67 semester credits

This conversion is part of LSAC’s official Credential Assembly Service (CAS) standardization process.

What to Do If Your LSAC GPA Is Low

A low LSAC CAS GPA is not the end of your law school prospects. Admissions decisions are holistic and heavily weighted on additional factors beyond GPA alone.

1. Focus on LSAT Score

Your undergraduate GPA is fixed once submitted to LSAC. However, your LSAT score remains fully controllable and is often the strongest compensating factor in admissions decisions.

  • Your GPA cannot be changed after graduation
  • Your LSAT performance can significantly offset a low GPA

2. Write a GPA Addendum

A GPA addendum is a short explanatory statement submitted with your application to clarify academic performance issues.

Common areas to address:

  • Academic challenges or disruptions
  • GPA inconsistencies or unusual grading patterns
  • Improvement trends in later semesters

3. Apply Strategically

Compare your LSAC GPA against the admission statistics of your target law schools.

  • 25th percentile GPA benchmarks
  • 75th percentile GPA benchmarks

Strategic targeting improves admission probability even with a below-median GPA.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does LSAC count retaken courses?

Yes. LSAC includes both attempts of a course regardless of institutional grade replacement policies. This means both the failed and repeated grade are factored into the CAS GPA calculation.

Can LSAC GPA exceed 4.0?

Yes. LSAC uses a 4.33 scale for A+ grades. Because of this, applicants with A+ grades can have a CAS GPA above 4.0.

Do law schools use LSAC GPA?

Yes. Law schools use the LSAC CAS GPA as the standardized GPA for admissions decisions rather than individual transcript GPAs.

Do withdrawals affect GPA?

Withdrawals are treated differently depending on type:

  • Non-punitive withdrawals (W, WP): Excluded from GPA calculation
  • Punitive withdrawals (WF, WU): Counted as 0.00 in GPA calculation
Does graduate school count toward LSAC GPA?

No. LSAC GPA only includes undergraduate coursework completed before the first bachelor’s degree is awarded. Graduate-level coursework is excluded from CAS GPA calculations.
